Synopsys 

Señora Carrar, is recently widowed by the civil war: her husband has been killed in combat by Franco’s soldiers, and now she’s determined to forbid their two sons of following the same path. Señora Carrar won’t tolerate any more emotional loss and is convinced that her family must completely withdraw from battle. Until Señora’s Carrar brother arrives on her doorstep. He has joined the anti-fascist militias: he comes to get the rifles that are hidden in Carrar’s household. Brother and sister battle over the consequences of withholding the rifles, until bloody events leads them to agree on the necessary course of action...

Themes

Brecht poses in *Rifles:*

can pacifism be an *absolute ethical value,* to be defended in all circumstances, with no possible exception? 

 With the rise of fascism around the world and the indiscriminate attack, murder and disappearance of citizens through state sanction terrorism around the globe , Rifle’s asks is being “neutral” a wise act of survival  or a fallacy  \- a cowardly mask to hide behind? 

***Director’s Statement – Leticia Caceres***

***Señora Carrar’s Rifles*** **is the sister play to *The  Drover’s Wife*.  Set in one location, over one tragic night, as the fascist regime closes in on a tiny Andalucian fishing village, Teresa Carrar is desperate to protect her two sons from a losing battle. Adamant she can prevent more senseless deaths; she chooses to disengage from the civil war that’s encroaching.  If she’s remains neutral, she is convinced the fascists will not come for family. But they come anyway and murder her eldest boy.** 

**Brecht rejected  *Señora Carrar’s Rifles* \- he felt its emotional weight was too powerful and clouded its political message.  I believe in this day and age, with so much political abstraction, audiences are craving for work that is emotionally arresting and grapples with urgent issues embodied by characters in a visceral way. That’s what this play offers and that’s what I can bring to the Belvoir stage: unforgettable, heartfelt performances, wrestling with moral, ethical and political questions that have genuine consequences for us all.** 

**Despite this play having had productions across the Americas and Europe*, Señora Carrar’s Rifles* has never had an Australian production. I would love to premier this work at Upstairs Belvoir. *Rifles* needs a venue that offers immediacy and intimacy and isn’t afraid to present potent and difficult themes and questions.**
