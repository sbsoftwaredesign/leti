**Artistic Statement /Creative Approach**  
*Leticia Caceres*

Bean is a coming-of-age romantic comedy about big dreams, big choices, big distances and tiny secrets. 

**Project History**   
Bean was first conceived and developed in 2015 by TV screenwriter Claire Phillips. Claire’s screenplay draws heavily on her own experience growing up in the small town of Dubbo, NSW. As the daughter of a single mother, Claire was fundamentally terrified about falling pregnant. Claire eventually did fall pregnant – albeit, in her mid-30s, and after giving birth to her son in 2020, she had a new understanding of the story and a refreshed commitment to writing the screenplay.

Claire wrote the screenplay on spec. On completing a second draft she showed her agent at Cameron’s Management. Her agent suggested attaching a director. At this point, Claire had become aware of me as a TV director via the SBS anthology series Erotic Stories. Claire was attracted to my work for both my ability to ground emotion and comedy, so she sent me her script. I was deeply moved by Claire’s story and voice. We instantly became a team.

Shortly after, we approached producer Miriam Stein from Tama Films. Both Claire and I were fans of Miriam’s film Hey, Hey, It’s Esther Blueburger. Miriam responded to Bean enthusiastically. On meeting, we knew we had a shared sensibility for the project. 

Since then, we have secured development funding from Screen NSW for Claire to write a third draft polish with the goal of attracting marquee talent, marketplace partners and financiers. 

**Themes**  
The subject of Abortion has been explored in cinema in very powerful ways. Gone are the days when characters conveniently miscarry or happily give up the baby for adoption. However, not all depictions of terminations are positive and continue to reinforce stigma on reproductive care. 

Terminations makes for compelling drama because they are still taboo. Through Bean, I hope to destigmatize abortions and advance the conversation on reproductive health, highlighting its continued barriers and the importance of access, particularly for young, rural people. However, this film will not be didactic. Rather, Bean will feel heartwarming, heartbreaking, triumphant and romantic. I want to take the audience on a coming-of-age story where the stakes are very high and the necessity for growth is paramount but where the audience still recognize the pitfalls of being young and naïve and incapable of asking for help when they need it most. Bean will embrace and celebrate with passion and humor, the power of friendship and young love, and the urgent need we have for an understanding parent. 

**Creative approach**   
In classic Australian road movie style, the vast Australian landscape will feature prominently in **Bean**. It will have a strong interplay between the vehicle and camera, creating a dynamic cinematic spectacle that celebrates movement and speed and complements the energy of youth. Its evolving landscape \- from rural to metropolitan- will be photographed in a way that will enhance the odyssey of our character’s journey. We will interweave dynamic driving montages to celebrate the thrill of the open road, and the great expanse of the Australian landscape juxtaposed with long shots to capture the theme of escape as our characters travers the country. Through the evolution of climates, from dry and brittle to coastal, to urban, Bean will speak to the themes of transformation and discovery, as Jem and Harry face grown-up decisions that will change them forever. 

Bean is unquestionably a vehicle (excuse the pun) for brilliant actors and well-crafted performances and not every scene will require virtuosic camera movement.  As a director with a twenty-year career in theatre, my emphasis will be on directing our young cast to deliver nuanced, delicate and truthful performances. When our scenes are concentrated on our character’s relationship, I will rely on handheld camera that frames our characters in dirty shots with shallow focus, to melt the exterior world away and keep them (and the audience) feeling united against a hostile world. I hope to marry the expanse and thrill of the road movie with the intimacy and humanity of a handheld style and create a film that feels both fresh and visually exiting but also grounded and full of heart. 

**Socio Political context**  
Although abortion is legal in Australia, in recent years we have seen an unprecedented attack on people’s reproductive rights, culminating in the historic overturning of *Roe V Wade* in the US by the Supreme Court.   
As one of the largest countries on earth, gynecological care has always been a struggle for many Australians. And due to the fact abortion rights are still not embedded in the public health system, doctors still have the right to conscientiously object to abortions and contraception if they so choose.

**Why Bean**  
Today young people experience an unprecedented level of pressure regarding personal choices, mental health, and societal expectations. With the rise of social media, young people often feel compelled to curate their lives to align with idealized standards, leading to feelings of inadequacy and isolation. Bean captures the authentic struggles of a young woman's journey towards self-acceptance, emphasizing the importance of open communication and support systems in overcoming personal challenges. 

Now more than ever, stories like Bean are vital in encouraging conversations about the  
complexities of adolescence. They can help reduce stigma, promote healthy relationships,  
and inspire young viewers to confront their own challenges with courage and authenticity.  
By resonating with their experiences and emotions, I hope Bean can empower a generation to  
embrace their true selves and navigate the turbulent waters of growing up with resilience.
