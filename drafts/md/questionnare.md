**What is your primary goal as a filmmaker? (maximum 200 words) // FR**

My primary goal as a storyteller is to make us more human. I believe films have the power to foster empathy, deepen emotional understanding, and encourage reflection on our shared human condition. I believe films go beyond mere entertainment and can be views as political acts of resistance.  I believe wholeheartedly in the power of films to change the world. I am especially passionate about telling female centric stories, and stories about outsiders and belonging.


**With which directors' work do you feel the strongest connection, and why? (**maximum 200 words)

I feel a deep kinship with the work of Brazilian director Walter Sallers. From *Motorcycle Diaries*, *Central Station* to *I’m Still Here*, he’s films are cinematic, truthful, delicate, grounded, epic and deeply affecting. His humanizes urgent issues embodied by characters in a visceral, faithful way. He’s collaboration with actors, the freedom he engenders in their work creates characters that are nuanced, authentic and unforgettable. His capacity to meld collective memory with personal narratives is inspiring, allowing audiences to experience the familiar in new light. His documentary background elicits powerful intimacy on screen. He has a profound respect for actors and is deeply collaborative with them.  His films provide a poignant counter-narrative to indifference, highlighting the human capacity for love and resistance.

**What are your top three favourite films and why?** (maximum 200 words)

*A Separation* written and directed by Asghar Farhadi – it resonates deeply with me as migrant.  Asghar elicits precise, nuanced performances from this character driven drama. Superbly written, with surprising turning points across every act. I adore the cinematographic style – the long, sustained takes made dynamic by blocking. Limited cuts maintain tension that doesn’t let up until its heartbreaking conclusion. 

*Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind* directed by Michel Gondry– romantic, painful, playful, humorous, and theatrical. It speaks to me as a filmmaker and a theatremaker. I adore the interplay between actors and the mise en scene – the use of lighting, set scale, colour- creating a powerful visual representation of memory.  The balance between comedy and drama is inspired. One of the most original depictions of the pain of love loss (and won).    

*Mustang* directed by Deniz Gamze Ergüven. I love its powerful depiction of female rebellion against a deeply conservative society.  Despite it being wholly rooted in Turkish culture, it resonates for women the world over. The struggle of the characters is made palpable through the use of restless camera work. How Ergüven builds tension to boiling point is masterful.
