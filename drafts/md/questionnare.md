**With which directors' work do you feel the strongest connection, and why? (**maximum 200 words)

I feel a deep kinship with the work of Brazilian director Walter Sallers. From _Motorcycle Diaries_, _Central Station_ to _I’m Still Here_, he’s films are cinematic, truthful, delicate, grounded, epic and deeply affecting. His humanizes urgent issues embodied by characters in a visceral, faithful way. He’s collaboration with actors, the freedom he engenders in their work creates characters that are nuanced, authentic and unforgettable. His capacity to meld collective memory with personal narratives is inspiring, allowing audiences to experience the familiar in new light. His documentary background elicits powerful intimacy on screen. He has a profound respect for actors and is deeply collaborative with them. His films provide a poignant counter-narrative to indifference, highlighting the human capacity for love and resistance.

**What are your top three favourite films and why?** (maximum 200 words)

_A Separation_ written and directed by Asghar Farhadi – it resonates deeply with me as migrant. Asghar elicits precise, nuanced performances from this character driven drama. Superbly written, with surprising turning points across every act. I adore the cinematographic style – the long, sustained takes made dynamic by blocking. Limited cuts maintain tension that doesn’t let up until its heartbreaking conclusion.

_Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind_ directed by Michel Gondry– romantic, painful, playful, humorous, and theatrical. It speaks to me as a filmmaker and a theatremaker. I adore the interplay between actors and the mise en scene – the use of lighting, set scale, colour- creating a powerful visual representation of memory. The balance between comedy and drama is inspired. One of the most original depictions of the pain of love loss (and won).

_Mustang_ directed by Deniz Gamze Ergüven. I love its powerful depiction of female rebellion against a deeply conservative society. Despite it being wholly rooted in Turkish culture, it resonates for women the world over. The struggle of the characters is made palpable through the use of restless camera work. How Ergüven builds tension to boiling point is masterful.

Why did you choose this specific clip? (maximum 200 words)

Weight Limit is a character driven short I wrote and directed in 2025. The scenes selected illustrate a mother -daughter relationship. Scene one highlights the intimacy and close bond between them. It highlights how a mother masks her anxiety and grief about moving to Japan to calm her child. This scene was shot handheld, in very intimate close ups, with a very shallow depth of field. It demonstrates my skills directing natural, believable screen performances. The second scene is more dynamic. The mother, unable to mask her fear of the immanent relocation has a shocking panic attack at her farewell party and flees. Her child breaks into a chase and finds the language to sooth her mum. These scenes are thematic mirrors, highlighting each character's attempt to soothe the apprehension the other feels about relocating. The mother helps the daughter in scene one, the daughter, helps the mother in scene two. Technically, the second scene demonstrates a new camera technique - the running sequence was shot on a Polaris, to capture frenetic movement, speed, and rhythm. We intercut between lead and follows, close ups, medium wides and insert shots of running feet. Both scenes reflect truthful, nuanced, emotionally charged performances.
