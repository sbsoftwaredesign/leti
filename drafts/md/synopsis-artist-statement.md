Logline
When precocious sixteen-year-old Jem discovers she's pregnant in her tiny Australian
country town, she convinces her best friend Harry to steal his mum's car and drive her to
Sydney for an abortion, but the journey across a vast and unfamiliar landscape forces both
of them to confront what they really feel, for themselves and for each other.
Synopsis
Precocious sixteen-year-old JEM is determined to escape her small Australian country
town. The only daughter of CHRISTINE, a single mum who fell pregnant as a teen, Jem is
terrified of repeating her mother's mistake. So, when she discovers she's pregnant, her
world collapses.
Jem hatches a plan. She turns up on best friend HARRY's doorstep, convinces him to steal
his mum's beat-up 2005 Toyota Tarago and drive her to Sydney for an abortion. They lie to
their parents and hit the road, but the journey isn’t easy. The Tarago is barely roadworthy,
and they've been forced to bring Christine's beloved pet rabbit, who's sick and needs
constant care.
Short on cash for the termination, Jem calls BOMBER, the boarding-school jock she had a
summer fling with, and the baby's “oops” father. When sparks reignite between Jem and
Bomer, Harry feels jealous, revealing feelings he's kept hidden. Bomer doesn’t take Jem’s
news well, rejecting her.
After the Tarago breaks down for good, Jem and Harry torch it to cover their tracks. In the
charged aftermath, they kiss, but instead of bringing them closer, it terrifies Jem.
Overwhelmed, she pushes Harry away, convinced she's ruined everything.
When Christine discovers Jem has been lying, she travels to Sydney to find her. Jem faces
her hardest choice: tell her mum the truth and ask for the help she desperately needs or
face the abortion alone. Christine learns the truth. She affirms for Jem that her body
autonomy is hers and her love is unconditional.

Artist Statement
My Path to Bean
I am a director with over twenty years of experience working across theatre, television, and short
film. Bean will be my feature film debut.
My career has been shaped by a deep commitment to stories that centre women, that illuminate
social concerns, and demand emotionally truthful performances. On stage, I have directed over
thirty professional productions for Australia's leading companies, including Leah Purcell's The
Drover's Wife at Belvoir St Theatre, which won the Helpmann Award for Best Direction and Best
Production. This collaboration led Leah to invite me to complete a director's attachment on her
feature film The Drover's Wife: The Legend of Molly Johnson, where I gained first-hand experience of
the creative and logistical demands of feature filmmaking. A second attachment followed with
director Michael Rymer (Battlestar Galactica) on acclaimed TV series The Fires for ABC, supported
by Film Victoria.
For television, I have directed across drama, comedy, and children's programming for Stan, SBS,
ABC, Network Seven, Nickelodeon, and BBC earning nominations for AACTA, LOGIE, and ADG
Awards. My episodes of SBS's Erotic Stories drew critical praise for their tonal control, balancing
emotional vulnerability with humour and sensuality, skills directly transferable to Bean.
When screenwriter Claire Phillips sent me her script, I instantly recognised a story I had been
waiting for: a coming-of-age romantic comedy with genuine dramatic stakes, a distinctly Australian
voice, and a young woman at its centre whose choices carry real consequence. Claire and I
partnered with producer Miriam Stein (Tama Films), whose work on Hey, Hey, It's Esther Blueburger
signalled a shared sensibility. We have since secured development funding from Screen NSW and
Claire is completing a third-draft polish with the goal of attracting marquee talent and marketplace
partners.
Creative Vision
Bean is a coming-of-age romantic comedy about big dreams, big choices, big distances, and tiny
secrets. It follows Jem, a sixteen-year-old determined to escape her small country town, who
discovers she is pregnant and convinces her best friend Harry to drive her to Sydney for an abortion.
Jem sets in motion a road trip that will test their friendship, ignite an unexpected romance, and
force them both to grow up fast.
In classic Australian road-movie tradition, the vast landscape will be a character in the film. I will
create a dynamic interplay between vehicle and camera: driving montages that celebrate the thrill
of the open road, wide shots that capture the theme of escape, and an evolving visual palette from
dry, brittle scrubland to coast and city that mirrors Jem and Harry's transformation. Films like Little

Miss Sunshine, Obvious Child, and Ladybird are touchstones: stories that marry scale with
intimacy, and comedy with genuine emotional jeopardy.
However, Bean is ultimately a performance-driven film, and this is where my theatre background
becomes my greatest asset. Twenty years of directing actors on stage have taught me how to build
trust, excavate subtext, and help young performers access complex emotional terrain. When our
scenes turn inward - Jem and Harry confronting problems beyond their lived experiences and
navigating feelings they cannot name - I will shift to handheld camera with shallow focus, framing
them in close, dirty shots that dissolve the exterior world and hold the audience inside the
relationship and allow for intimacy with our heroes. My goal with this film is for it to feel both
visually bold and deeply human.
Themes & Relevance
Through Bean, I want to destigmatise abortion and advance the conversation about reproductive
access, particularly for young, rural people. Although abortion is legal in Australia, access remains
starkly unequal: geographical distance, cost, and doctors' right to conscientious objection create
barriers that disproportionately affect regional communities. The overturning of Roe v. Wade in the
US has intensified global scrutiny of reproductive rights, making Bean's subject matter urgent and
resonant far beyond Australia.
Yet, Bean will not be a polemic. Its power lies in its warmth, humour, and emotional honesty. I want
the audience to fall in love with Jem and Harry, to feel the stakes of their choices not as political
abstractions but as a lived, visceral experience. I want young viewers to see their own struggles with
identity, pressure, and the terror of asking for help reflected on screen. Stories like Bean are vital in
encouraging open conversations about the complexities of adolescence, reducing stigma, and
empowering a generation to face their challenges with courage and authenticity.
Why TIFF Directors' Lab
Bean is at a pivotal stage: development funding has been secured, the script is evolving into a third
draft polish, and a strong creative team is in place. What we need now is the marketplace expertise
to take it from development into production. TIFF Directors' Lab's focus on market readiness,
combined with mentorship from directors who have navigated their own debuts, is precisely the
catalyst this project needs. The opportunity to refine my pitch, connect with international
distributors and financiers, and learn from the industry's best would be transformative for both
Bean and my career as a first-time feature director.
