Part 1 · Director's Statement — Analysis

Issue 1: Feature Debut Positioning Is Buried

TIFF Directors' Lab explicitly targets "creators embarking on their directorial debuts." The programme description uses this language prominently. Yet the statement never once says "this is my debut feature film." It's implied through the project history, but selectors reading hundreds of applications need this stated clearly and early.

Leticia's trajectory — 20+ years of theatre, award-winning TV, two feature attachments — tells a powerful story of deliberate preparation. That story needs to be told.

Open the statement with Leticia's identity as a director on the cusp of her feature debut, and position her extensive career as the foundation for that leap.

Issue 2: Too Much Screenwriter Backstory

Approximately 40% of the "Project History" section is about Claire Phillips — her background, her pregnancy, her agent, her motivations. While context is necessary, selectors want to hear Leticia's voice and vision. The application is for the director, not the writer.

Reduce project history to 2–3 sentences. Foreground what drew Leticia to the material and what she brings as director.

Issue 3: No Mention of Why TIFF Directors' Lab

The application never addresses why Leticia is applying to this programme specifically. TIFF Directors' Lab offers market readiness, mentorship from master directors, and festival industry access. Given that Bean has funding and a producer attached, articulating what the Lab would unlock (marketplace connections, mentorship on navigating the debut, distributor relationships) would demonstrate strategic thinking.

Add 2–3 sentences near the end connecting the Lab's offerings to Bean's current stage of development.

Issue 4: Thematic Redundancy

Three separate sections — "Themes," "Socio-Political Context," and "Why Bean" — cover overlapping ground about abortion stigma, reproductive rights, and youth pressure. This inflates the word count and diffuses the argument.

Consolidate into a single "Themes & Relevance" section that makes the case once, powerfully.

Issue 5: Theatre-to-Screen Transition Is Undersold

Leticia's single biggest differentiator is her actor-directing depth — 30+ professional stage productions, including work with Leah Purcell (who then invited her onto her feature), Helpmann Awards for direction. This is mentioned in one sentence ("As a director with a twenty-year career in theatre"). The statement should demonstrate how this experience specifically informs her approach to Bean.

Expand the connection between theatre craft and screen directing. Emphasize the skill of creating intimate, performance-driven moments — exactly what a coming-of-age film needs.

Issue 6: Minor Language Issues

"travers" → "traverse" · "exiting" → "exciting" · Comma splices in several long sentences · "Terminations makes" → subject-verb disagreement.

All corrected in the revised version below.

Part 2 · Director's Statement — Revised Version

Restructured to lead with directorial identity and debut positioning. Consolidated themes. Shortened project history, foregrounded vision. Added TIFF Lab connection. Trimmed to fit 2-page A4 max.

Revised — Director's Statement

My Path to Bean

I am a director with over twenty years of experience across theatre, television, and short film — and Bean will be my feature debut.

My career has been shaped by a commitment to stories that centre women, illuminate social fault lines, and demand emotionally truthful performances. On stage, I have directed over thirty professional productions for Australia's leading companies — including Leah Purcell's The Drover's Wife at Belvoir St Theatre, which won the Helpmann Award for Best Direction and Best Production. That collaboration led Leah to invite me as Director's Attachment on her feature film The Drover's Wife: The Legend of Molly Johnson, where I gained first-hand experience of the creative and logistical demands of feature filmmaking. A second attachment followed with Michael Rymer on The Fires for ABC.

In television, I have directed across drama, comedy, and children's programming for Stan, SBS, ABC, Network Seven, Nickelodeon, and BBC — earning nominations for AACTA, LOGIE, and ADG Awards. My episodes of SBS's Erotic Stories drew critical praise for their tonal control, balancing emotional vulnerability with humour and sensuality — skills directly transferable to Bean.

When screenwriter Claire Phillips sent me her script, I recognised a story I had been waiting for: a coming-of-age romantic comedy with genuine dramatic stakes, a distinctly Australian voice, and a young woman at its centre whose choices carry real consequence. Claire and I partnered with producer Miriam Stein (Tama Films), whose work on Hey, Hey, It's Esther Blueburger signalled a shared sensibility. We have since secured development funding from Screen NSW, and Claire is completing a third-draft polish with the goal of attracting marquee talent and marketplace partners.

Creative Vision

Bean is a coming-of-age romantic comedy about big dreams, big choices, big distances, and tiny secrets. It follows Jem, a sixteen-year-old determined to escape her small country town, who discovers she is pregnant and convinces her best friend Harry to drive her to Sydney for an abortion — setting in motion a road trip that will test their friendship, ignite unexpected romance, and force them both to grow up.

In classic Australian road-movie tradition, the vast landscape will be a character in the film. I will create a dynamic interplay between vehicle and camera — driving montages that celebrate the thrill of the open road, wide shots that capture the theme of escape, and an evolving visual palette from dry, brittle scrubland to coast and city that mirrors Jem and Harry's transformation. Films like Thelma & Louise, Juno, and The Edge of Seventeen are touchstones: stories that marry scale with intimacy, and comedy with genuine emotional danger.

But Bean is ultimately a performance-driven film, and this is where my theatre background becomes my greatest asset. Twenty years of directing actors on stage have taught me how to build trust, excavate subtext, and help young performers access complex emotional terrain. When our scenes turn inward — Jem and Harry alone in the car, navigating feelings they cannot name — I will shift to handheld camera with shallow focus, framing them in close, dirty shots that dissolve the exterior world and hold the audience inside the relationship. The goal is a film that feels both visually bold and deeply human.

Themes & Relevance

Through Bean, I want to destigmatise abortion and advance the conversation about reproductive access — particularly for young, rural people. Although abortion is legal in Australia, access remains starkly unequal: geographical distance, cost, and doctors' right to conscientious objection create barriers that disproportionately affect regional communities. The overturning of Roe v. Wade in the US has intensified global scrutiny of reproductive rights, making Bean's subject matter urgent and resonant far beyond Australia.

But Bean will not be a polemic. Its power lies in its warmth, humour, and emotional honesty. I want the audience to fall in love with Jem and Harry, to feel the stakes of their choices not as political abstractions but as lived, visceral experience. I want young viewers to see their own struggles with identity, pressure, and the terror of asking for help reflected on screen — and to feel less alone. Stories like Bean are vital in encouraging open conversation about the complexities of adolescence, reducing stigma, and empowering a generation to face their challenges with courage and authenticity.

Why TIFF Directors' Lab

Bean is at a pivotal stage: the script is in its third draft, development funding is secured, and a strong creative team is in place. What we need now is the marketplace expertise to take it from development into production. TIFF Directors' Lab's focus on market readiness — combined with mentorship from directors who have navigated their own debuts — is precisely the catalyst this project requires. The opportunity to refine my pitch, connect with international distributors and financiers, and learn from the industry's best would be transformative for both Bean and my career as a first-time feature director.

Part 3 · Logline — Analysis

Current Version

Faced with an unplanned pregnancy, two teenagers set out on the road together, only to discover what they are truly seeking is each other.

25 words (limit: 50)

Generic

This could describe a film set anywhere, in any era. There's no sense of the Australian setting, the rural-to-urban journey, or the specific genre (romantic comedy with dramatic stakes).

Clichéd Closing

"Discover what they are truly seeking is each other" is a well-worn formula. It also undersells the story — the film isn't just about romance; it's about reproductive choice, growing up, and the courage to ask for help.

Underuses Word Budget

At 25 words against a 50-word limit, there's significant room to add specificity, flavour, and stakes.

Revised Logline

When precocious sixteen-year-old Jem discovers she's pregnant in her tiny Australian country town, she convinces her best friend Harry to steal his mum's car and drive her to Sydney for an abortion — but the journey across a vast and unforgiving landscape forces both of them to confront what they really feel, for themselves and for each other.

49 words ✓
  • Added character name and age (specificity)
  • Established Australian setting and rural-to-urban geography
  • Included the car theft (raises stakes, adds genre flavour)
  • "Vast and unforgiving landscape" signals road-movie genre visually
  • Dual transformation: self-discovery + romance
  • Removed the cliché

Part 4 · Synopsis — Analysis

Current Version

Precocious 16-year-old JEM is determined to make it out of her small country town. As the only daughter to CHRISTINE, a single mum and a cleaner, who fell pregnant with Jem when she was still a teen, Jem is determined to avoid her mum's mistake – which is why she's horrified to discover she's pregnant.

Jem hashes a plan. She rocks up on her best friend HARRY's doorstep and convinces him to steal his mum's beat up 2005 Toyota Tarago and drive her to Sydney to have an abortion.

Jem and Harry lie to their parents and hit the road. But they quickly discover this road trip isn't gonna be easy. Not only is the Tarago barely roadworthy– but they've been forced to bring along Christine's beloved pet rabbit, who's sick and requires critical care nursing.

Jem needs cash for the abortion, so she reluctantly calls the "oops" baby's father - BOMBER, the boarding school jock she had a fling with over summer. But as sparks reignite between them, Harry feels jealous.

When Jem and Harry are forced to torch the Tarago to cover their tracks, their true feelings come to the surface, and they kiss. But instead of bringing them together, it forces them apart. Jem, scared, confused, leaves Harry, adamant she's blown her life up.

Jem's mum discovers Jem's been lying to her. She travels to Sydney to bring Jem home forcing Jem to make a choice –tell her mum the truth or have the abortion alone.

~230 words (limit: 250)

What Works

  • Strong character setup: Jem's background, Christine, generational fear
  • Escalating stakes: roadworthiness, the rabbit, Bomber, Harry's jealousy
  • The sick rabbit is a terrific comic/emotional device — a surrogate for care and responsibility
  • Clear genre signals: comedy, romance, road movie

What Needs Work

  • Typo: "hashes a plan" → "hatches a plan"
  • Ending is abrupt — no hint of emotional resolution or payoff
  • Why they torch the Tarago is unexplained — needs brief context
  • Bomber appears then vanishes — his function as catalyst needs clarity

Revised Synopsis

Precocious sixteen-year-old JEM is determined to escape her small Australian country town. The only daughter of CHRISTINE — a single mum and cleaner who fell pregnant as a teen — Jem is terrified of repeating her mother's path. So when she discovers she's pregnant, her world collapses.

Jem hatches a plan. She turns up on best friend HARRY's doorstep and convinces him to steal his mum's beat-up 2005 Toyota Tarago and drive her to Sydney for an abortion. They lie to their parents and hit the road — but the journey is anything but easy. The Tarago is barely roadworthy, and they've been forced to bring Christine's beloved pet rabbit, who's sick and needs constant care.

Desperate for cash, Jem calls BOMBER, the boarding-school jock she had a summer fling with — and the baby's father. When sparks reignite between them, Harry's jealousy surfaces, revealing feelings he's kept hidden. After the Tarago breaks down for good, Jem and Harry torch it to cover their tracks. In the charged aftermath, they kiss — but instead of bringing them closer, it terrifies Jem. Scared and overwhelmed, she pushes Harry away, convinced she's ruined everything.

When Christine discovers Jem has been lying and travels to Sydney to bring her home, Jem faces her hardest choice: tell her mum the truth and ask for the help she desperately needs, or face the abortion alone.

  • Added brief context for torching the Tarago (breaks down for good)
  • Clarified Harry's hidden feelings as the driver of his jealousy
  • Refined the ending to land on the emotional question while implying growth
  • Tightened prose throughout

Delivery Tips
