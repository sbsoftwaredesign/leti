# Señora Carrar's Rifles

_By Bertolt Brecht · Directed by Leticia Cáceres_

## Synopsis

Spain, 1937. In a cramped fishing cottage on the Andalucian coast, **TERESA CARRAR** stands vigil. Her husband was killed fighting Franco's forces, and she has sworn that her two sons will not share his fate. She hides the family's rifles, bars the door, and refuses every appeal from neighbours who beg her to join the resistance. If she stays silent, she reasons, the fascists will pass her by.

Then her brother **PEDRO** arrives. A militiaman, bloodied and urgent, he has come for those rifles. What follows is a fierce, breathless confrontation between two people who love each other but cannot agree on the price of survival. Pedro argues that neutrality is complicity; Teresa insists that one more death will not save Spain. Their debate tears open every wound the war has inflicted on this family: grief, guilt, rage, and the paralysing fear that action will only multiply loss.

The argument is still raging when Teresa's eldest son is carried home, murdered by the soldiers she believed would spare him. In a single devastating moment, every conviction she held collapses. Teresa retrieves the rifles herself, arms her remaining son, and walks out into the night to fight.

## Themes

Brecht's question is searingly simple: can pacifism hold when the enemy recognises no rules? _Señora Carrar's Rifles_ asks whether neutrality in the face of fascism is a principled stand or a dangerous delusion; a refuge or a trap.

With authoritarian movements resurging across the globe and state-sanctioned violence escalating from Latin America to Eastern Europe, the play's central provocation has lost none of its force. Brecht wrote it in 1937 as an urgent response to the Spanish Civil War. Nearly ninety years later, audiences are still living the question: when does standing aside become standing with the oppressor?

## Director's Statement

### A Sister Play to _The Drover's Wife_

_Señora Carrar's Rifles_ is the natural companion to my production of Leah Purcell's _The Drover's Wife_ at Belvoir St Theatre, which won the Helpmann Award for Best Direction and Best Production. Both plays confine a woman to a single location over one night of escalating threat, and both demand that she choose between the safety of withdrawal and the risk of action. Directing _The Drover's Wife_ taught me how to build relentless tension within a confined space, how to let silence operate as a dramatic force, and how to anchor a political argument in one woman's body and voice. I want to bring all of that craft to _Rifles_.

### Why This Play, Why Now

Despite productions across the Americas and Europe, _Señora Carrar's Rifles_ has never been staged in Australia. That absence feels increasingly glaring. The play speaks directly to debates Australians are having right now: about complicity and conscience, about the obligations of democratic citizens when institutions fail. As the daughter of Argentine migrants who lived through state terrorism, I carry a personal understanding of what happens when ordinary people convince themselves that silence will keep them safe. That lived knowledge will inform every choice I make in this production, from the casting to the design of the space.

### Staging and Approach

I envision a stripped-back, claustrophobic production in Upstairs Belvoir, a venue whose intimacy is essential to this play. The audience must feel trapped alongside Teresa, sealed inside her kitchen while the war presses against the walls. The set will be minimal: a table, chairs, the hidden rifles, and a doorway that becomes the play's most charged threshold, the boundary between private conviction and public action.

Lighting will shift from warm, domestic lamplight to the cold intrusion of the outside world as Pedro's arguments and the sounds of conflict erode Teresa's defences. Sound design will be critical: distant gunfire, a radio bulletin, the sea; each element tightening the pressure until the final, shattering knock at the door.

I plan to cast Latina Australian actors in the central roles, grounding the production in specific cultural experience and connecting the Spanish Civil War setting to the diasporic histories present in contemporary Australia. Brecht wrote this play to move audiences to political feeling; I intend to honour that impulse by making the performers' heritage, breath, and physical presence the production's primary instruments.

### What Brecht Got Wrong (and What We Can Reclaim)

Brecht famously disowned _Rifles_, believing its emotional power overwhelmed his political argument. I believe that tension is precisely the play's strength. In an era saturated with political abstraction, audiences are hungry for work that embodies urgent questions in human terms: characters whose choices carry genuine, visceral consequences. That is what this play offers, and that is what I can bring to the Belvoir stage: unforgettable, heartfelt performances wrestling with moral and ethical questions that matter to us all.
