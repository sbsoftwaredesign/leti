## Creative Questions

### How would TIFF Directors' Lab benefit you at this time in your career, and at this stage of development in your current project?

_(maximum 200 words)_

I am at the point of making my feature film debut. After 20 years directing over 30 theatre productions and multiple seasons of critically acclaimed television, I have the creative skills and industry experience to deliver _Bean_; what I need now is the marketplace expertise to take it from development into production.

The Lab would provide essential mentorship to refine my pitch, develop my lookbook, and build a financing strategy with guidance from directors who have navigated their own debuts. Working with international mentors and peers would sharpen my understanding of how to position a distinctly Australian story for global audiences.

_Bean_ is at a pivotal stage: development funding from Screen NSW is secured, Claire Phillips is completing a third-draft polish, and producer Miriam Stein (Tama Films) is attached. The project has strong commercial and social relevance, exploring reproductive access through the lens of a coming-of-age road movie. What we need now is connection to distributors, sales agents, and financiers to move towards production.

Participating in the Lab would also provide invaluable validation for an emerging feature director, reinforcing to the industry that my voice, vision, and project are worth investing in.

### What is your primary goal as a filmmaker?

_(maximum 200 words)_

My primary goal as a filmmaker is to create stories that make us more human. I believe films have the power to foster empathy, deepen emotional understanding, and encourage reflection on our shared condition. Films can be political acts of resistance, and I believe wholeheartedly in their capacity to change the world.

I am especially passionate about telling female-centric stories that centre outsiders, belonging, and the quiet acts of courage that define ordinary lives. My theatre background has given me a deep understanding of the actor's process, and I bring this to screen: performances that feel raw, truthful, and emotionally precise. In _Erotic Stories_ for SBS, I balanced emotional vulnerability with humour and sensuality. In _Bump_ for Stan, I found authentic teen voices navigating complex family dynamics. These skills are central to my vision for _Bean_, where the performances of two young leads must carry the emotional weight of an entire road movie.

I want to make films that stay with audiences, that provoke conversation, and give visibility to experiences too often overlooked. Through _Bean_, I aim to destigmatise abortion and champion reproductive access for young, rural people through warmth, humour, and emotional honesty rather than polemic.

### With which directors' work do you feel the strongest connection, and why?

_(maximum 200 words)_

I feel a deep kinship with the work of Brazilian director Walter Salles. From _The Motorcycle Diaries_ and _Central Station_ to _I'm Still Here_, his films are cinematic, truthful, delicate, grounded, epic, and deeply affecting. He humanises urgent issues through characters embodied in a visceral, faithful way. His collaboration with actors and the freedom he engenders in their work creates characters that are nuanced, authentic, and unforgettable. His capacity to meld collective memory with personal narratives allows audiences to experience the familiar in new light.

Salles's documentary background elicits powerful intimacy on screen; he has a profound respect for actors and is deeply collaborative with them. His films provide a poignant counter-narrative to indifference, highlighting the human capacity for love and resistance.

This resonates with my own approach. Like Salles, I come to cinema from a background (theatre rather than documentary) that prizes the actor's experience above all. I share his commitment to stories rooted in specific places and cultures that nonetheless speak universally. _Bean_, like his best work, uses a journey across landscape as both physical adventure and emotional reckoning.

### What are your top three favourite films and why?

_(maximum 200 words)_

_A Separation_ written and directed by Asghar Farhadi: it resonates deeply with me as a migrant. Farhadi elicits precise, nuanced performances from this character-driven drama. Superbly written, with surprising turning points across every act. I adore the cinematographic style: long, sustained takes made dynamic by blocking. Limited cuts maintain tension that does not let up until its heartbreaking conclusion.

_Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind_ directed by Michel Gondry: romantic, painful, playful, humorous, and theatrical. It speaks to me as a filmmaker and a theatremaker. I adore the interplay between actors and the mise en scène, the use of lighting, set scale, and colour creating a powerful visual representation of memory. The balance between comedy and drama is inspired. One of the most original depictions of the pain of love lost (and won).

_Mustang_ directed by Deniz Gamze Ergüven. I love its powerful depiction of female rebellion against a deeply conservative society. Despite being wholly rooted in Turkish culture, it resonates for women the world over. The struggle of the characters is made palpable through restless camera work. How Ergüven builds tension to boiling point is masterful.

### Logline

When precocious sixteen-year-old Jem discovers she is pregnant in her tiny Australian country town, she convinces her best friend Harry to steal his mum's car and drive her to Sydney for an abortion. ### Synopsis

_(maximum 200 words)_

Precocious sixteen-year-old **JEM** is determined to escape her small Australian country town. The only daughter of **CHRISTINE**, a single mum who fell pregnant as a teen, Jem is terrified of repeating her mother's mistake. When she discovers she is pregnant, her world collapses.

Jem convinces best friend **HARRY** to steal his mum's beat-up Toyota Tarago and drive her to Sydney for an abortion. They lie to their parents and hit the road, but the Tarago is barely roadworthy, and they must bring Christine's sick pet rabbit along for the ride.

Short on cash, Jem calls **BOMBER**, the boarding-school jock she had a summer fling with. When sparks reignite between Jem and Bomber, Harry's hidden feelings surface. Bomber rejects Jem. After the Tarago breaks down for good, Jem and Harry torch it and, in the charged aftermath, kiss; but instead of bringing them closer, it terrifies Jem.

When Christine discovers the truth, she travels to Sydney. Jem faces her hardest choice: tell her mum everything and ask for help, or face the abortion alone. Christine affirms that Jem's bodily autonomy is hers, and her love is unconditional.

### Director's Statement

_(maximum 200 words)_

When screenwriter Claire Phillips sent me her script, I instantly recognised a story I had been waiting for: a coming-of-age road movie with genuine dramatic stakes, a distinctly Australian voice, and a young woman at its centre whose choices carry real consequence. Through _Bean_, I want to destigmatise abortion and advance the conversation about reproductive access for young, rural people, not through polemic, but through warmth, humour, and emotional honesty.

In classic Australian road-movie tradition, the vast landscape will be a character in the film. I will create a dynamic interplay between vehicle and camera: driving montages that celebrate the thrill of the open road, wide shots that capture the theme of escape, and an evolving visual palette from dry, brittle scrubland to coast and city that mirrors Jem and Harry's transformation. _Little Miss Sunshine_, _Obvious Child_, and _Lady Bird_ are touchstones.

_Bean_ is ultimately a performance-driven film, and this is where my theatre background becomes my greatest asset. When our scenes turn inward, I will shift to handheld camera with shallow focus, framing the characters in close, intimate shots that dissolve the exterior world. My goal is for this film to feel both visually bold and deeply human.

### Clip Description

_(maximum 250 words: Please put the clip in context and explain where it is in the project.)_

Josefina has spent her life building a sanctuary in Australia since her emigration from Colombia. When she agrees to leave behind her community, identity, and hard-won stability for her husband Carl's career in Japan, she must mask her grief with a brave face for their daughter, Lucia. On the eve of their farewell party, the gravity of what she is losing becomes unbearable.

Haunted by her sacrifices of the past, Josefina's instinct to protect herself triggers a desperate escape. It is only when Lucia's cries pierce through her panic that Josefina stops fleeing. In a vulnerable moment, mother and daughter confront the magnitude of their displacement. Through Lucia's quiet reassurance, Josefina realises she is not carrying this weight alone, and finds the courage to embrace the next uncertain chapter.

A semi-autobiographical work by Argentine-Australian writer-director Leticia Cáceres, _Weight Limit_ is a poignant exploration of the migrant experience and the heavy emotional toll of starting over.

### Why did you choose this specific clip?

_(maximum 200 words)_

_Weight Limit_ is a character-driven short I wrote and directed in 2025. The scenes selected illustrate a mother-daughter relationship. The first scene highlights the intimacy and close bond between them, showing how a mother masks her anxiety and grief about moving to Japan to calm her child. This scene was shot handheld, in very intimate close-ups, with a very shallow depth of field. It demonstrates my skills directing natural, believable screen performances.

The second scene is more dynamic. The mother, unable to mask her fear of the imminent relocation, has a shocking panic attack at her farewell party and flees. Her child breaks into a chase and finds the language to soothe her mum. These scenes are thematic mirrors, highlighting each character's attempt to soothe the apprehension the other feels about relocating; the mother helps the daughter in scene one, and the daughter helps the mother in scene two.

Technically, the second scene demonstrates a new camera technique: the running sequence was shot on a Polaris to capture frenetic movement, speed, and rhythm. We intercut between lead and follow shots, close-ups, medium wides, and insert shots of running feet. Both scenes reflect truthful, nuanced, emotionally charged performances.
